Boeing 707

Operation

In October 1956 BOAC ordered 15 Boeing 707s with Conway engines (briefly the most economical commercial engine option). They entered service in 1960. (The British airworthiness authorities insisted on tail-fin modifications which Boeing made available to all 707 users.) Sir Giles Guthrie, who took charge of BOAC in 1964, preferred Boeing aircraft for economic reasons, and indeed BOAC began turning a profit in the late 1960s. BOAC flew routes with the 707 all over the world with routes across the Pacific via the US and into Asia from London and worked along side the Comet and later VC10 across the Atlantic, with service continuing into British Airways
