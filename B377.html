B377 Stratocruiser

Operations

BOAC first introduced the Stratocruiser in 1949 with the intension of flying them on cross atlantic routes. The B377 was fitted out with 50 first class seats charging $400 one way from New York to London, and 81 tourist class seats charging $290. The 377 was one of the most advanced and capable of the propeller-driven transports, and among the most luxurious at the time, beacuse of this BOAC fitted them with sleeping berths, and amongst other luxurious features, which you can find more information on. Flying across the atlantic was no easy feat nor a quick one below is an example of a BOAC timetable from 1951 showing the route the aircraft flew.

General characteristics

Crew: 4 flight crew + cabin crew
Capacity: Up to 100 passengers on main deck plus 14 in lower deck lounge; typical seating for 63 or 84 passengers or 28 berthed and five seated passengers.
Length: 110 ft 4 in (33.63 m)
Wingspan: 131 ft 3 in (40.01 m)
Height: 38 ft 3 in (11.66 m)
Wing area: 1,769 sq ft (164.3 m2)
Empty weight: 83,500 lb (37,875 kg)
Max takeoff weight: 148,000 lb (67,132 kg)
Powerplant: 4 × Pratt & Whitney R-4360-B6 Wasp Major 28-cylinder air-cooled radial piston engines, 3,500 hp (2,600 kW) each
Propellers: 4-bladed constant-speed fully-feathering propellers

Performance

Maximum speed: 375 mph (604 km/h, 326 kn)
Cruise speed: 301 mph (484 km/h, 262 kn) * Maximum cruise speed: 240 mph (210 kn; 390 km/h)
Range: 4,200 mi (6,800 km, 3,600 nmi)
Service ceiling: 32,000 ft (9,800 m)

Cockpit 

The Straocruiser was converted from military use to passenger use after the war so the technology onboard was very basic and was desinged to be used in a war time enviroment.  

Luxury

The B377 was the peak of luxury right afte the WW2 where very few people flew with the price being £18,144 in todays money to fly to Perth for example. Because of this nothing onboard was done by half there was large recliner chairs with plenty of leg room, full silver service with multi course meal, and even a lounge area on the bottom deck where passengers could socialise.     

Timetable

Above is an example of a route flown by the Stratocruiser. The timetable dates from 1951 and shows the stops the aircraft makes during it's atlantic crossing to America and Canada. With the aircraft at the time not having big enough fuel tanks to make the crossing non stop they stop at Shannon, Boston then the short hop to New York, with Canadian flights stopping at Prestwick for a quick refuel
