Boeing 314 Clipper Flying Boat

Operations

BOAC operated 3 of these aircraft on their route network with the registrations and names of, G-AGBZ (Bristol) G-AGCA(Berwick) G-AGCB (Bangor), between 1941 and 1948. The aircraft were used throughout BOAC's networking conecting distant parts of the empire to London stopping at many places on the way. BOAC retired these aircraft in January 1948 with the Baltimore to Bermuda route, it would be replaced by Lockheed Constellations.

General characteristics

 
Crew: 11, including 2 cabin stewards
Capacity: Daytime: 74 passengers, Nighttime: 36 passengers
Payload: 10,000 lb (4,500 kg) of mail and cargo
Length: 106 ft (32.33 m)
Wingspan: 152 ft (46.36 m)
Height: 20 ft 4½ in (6.22 m)
Empty weight: 48,400 lb (21,900 kg)
Loaded weight: 84,000 lb (38,000 kg)
Powerplant: 4 × Wright R-2600-3 radial engines, 1,600 hp (1,200 kW) each
 

Performance

 
Maximum speed: 210 mph (180 knots, 340 km/h)
Cruise speed: 188 mph (163 knots, 302 km/h) at 11,000 ft (3,400 m)
Range: 3,685 mi (3,201 nm, 5,896 km) normal cruise
Service ceiling: 19,600 ft (5,980 m)

Cockpit

Cockpits in the early days of commercial aviation were very basic with only the nessersary information being shown with other information given to the other officers in the cockpit. The flight controls are very different to the controls of today so much they resembled car steering wheels.

Navigation & radio stations

To help make these aircraft fly the vast distances they did they need a whole crew to fly it. A navigator to read maps and use a sextant to stay on course. Radio operator to run the radios and talk to air traffic control about the aircrafts intentions. Finally a engineer to moniter and operate the engines on the aircraft.
